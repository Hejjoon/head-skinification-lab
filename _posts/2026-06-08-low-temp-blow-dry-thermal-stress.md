You step out of a relaxing shower and plug in your hair dryer. To save time, you click it to the maximum heat setting. As the hot wind blasts your roots, your hair dries quickly, but your scalp begins to feel uncomfortably hot and dry. Soon, a tight, pulling sensation spreads across your crown, leaving your scalp feeling stiff and sensitized.

To speed up their routines, many people dry their hair with intense, high-heat settings, believing that wet hair is the only source of scalp problems. This is a common mistake. Exposing the delicate scalp to high thermal stress acts as a severe dehydrator, baking away the protective moisture barrier and causing structural tension in the skin tissues.

The scalp is a biological layer of living cells, not a inert fabric to be heat-treated. It requires moderate temperatures to preserve its hydration and structural elasticity.

This drying, tight feeling is the result of scalp thermal stress. High heat speeds up transepidermal water loss (TEWL) and causes the scalp's delicate collagen fibers to stiffen. Keeping the drying temperature under 38°C (using the cool or low-warm setting) protects the scalp barrier. Low-temperature drying gently removes excess water from the hair shaft while keeping the stratum corneum hydrated and preserving the elastic, supportive structure of the follicles.
