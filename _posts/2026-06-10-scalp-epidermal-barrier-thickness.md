You step into the bathroom and wash your hair, scrubbing aggressively to clear away sebum. You assume that because the scalp grows thick hair, the skin itself must be tough and resilient. However, a few hours later, you feel a lingering tightness and a subtle sting when you brush your hair. You wonder why your scalp feels so sensitive.

To fix this sensitive feeling, many people use harsh physical scrubs or deep-exfoliating chemical peels. They believe heavy exfoliation is required to keep the scalp clean. This is a severe misconception. The scalp's outermost protective layer, the stratum corneum, is actually much thinner and more fragile than facial skin. Aggressive scrubbing strips away the protective lipid boundary, leaving the scalp vulnerable to environmental stress.

The scalp is a delicate biological layer that requires gentle, skin-centric care. It needs to be protected, not stripped.

Biologically, the scalp's epidermal barrier is surprisingly thin—measuring only about 9㎛ in thickness, which is half the thickness of facial skin. When you use rigid brushes or harsh surfactants, you easily tear this delicate lipid layer. This causes micro-abrasions, allowing moisture to escape rapidly and triggering a defensive surge of sebum. Furthermore, if symptoms persist or worsen, consulting a dermatologist is highly recommended. Switching to gentle, hand-cleansing habits and wide-tooth wooden tools protects these cellular junctions.
