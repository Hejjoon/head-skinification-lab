You step out of the shower on a cold winter evening, immediately feeling the dry, artificial warmth of the room heater. As your hair begins to dry, a tight, pulling sensation spreads across your crown. By the next morning, that tightness shifts into dry itchiness, accompanied by tiny, fine white flakes drifting onto your shoulders when you brush your hair.

To combat this dry winter sensation, many people apply heavy hair oils or thick moisturizing conditioners directly to their roots. They assume that heavy coating will replenish the lost moisture. This is a common misconception. Heavy oils can clog follicles, trapping sweat underneath and creating a breed-ground for yeast, while doing nothing to repair the structural lipid barrier of the stratum corneum.

---

## The Winter Squeeze: Heater Air and Stratum Corneum Dehydration

### Q. Why does my scalp feel so tight and dry when the room heater is on?

**A.** Indoor heating systems release extremely dry air that rapidly lowers the humidity level of your environment. This low humidity creates a steep moisture gradient between your scalp and the air. 

Because the scalp’s stratum corneum is only 9㎛ thick—significantly thinner than your facial skin barrier—it lacks the structural depth to resist this dry gradient. <mark style="background: rgb(221, 235, 241);">**The dry air pulls moisture directly from the outer epidermal layers**</mark>, causing the dry skin cells to shrink and trigger a tight, uncomfortable pulling sensation.

### Q. How does washing my hair affect this tightness?

**A.** Washing with hot water and drying with a hot blow dryer strips away the natural sebum envelope and inter-cellular lipids (ceramides and fatty acids). 

<mark style="background: rgb(221, 235, 241);">**This leaves the thin 9㎛ barrier entirely exposed to the dry heater currents.**</mark> Without its natural lipid shield, the rate of water loss increases exponentially, causing the superficial cells to peel away as dry flakes.

---

## The Impact of Heat on Lipid Cohesion

### Q. How does high temperature drying damage the scalp barrier?

**A.** The lipids holding your stratum corneum cells together melt and reorganize when exposed to temperatures above 40°C. Using a hot blow dryer liquifies these protective lipids, washing them away or dispersing them, which ruins the barrier structure.

## The Solution: Under 38°C Low-Temperature Drying

### Q. How should I dry my hair in the winter to prevent scalp tightness?

**A.** The key is to dry your roots using only a low-temperature air setting under 38°C. Keep the nozzle of the dryer at least 15 to 20 centimeters away from your head, moving it constantly to avoid localized heat build-up. 

<mark style="background: rgb(221, 235, 241);">**This gentle airflow evaporates water from the hair shafts without melting the scalp’s lipid bilayers**</mark>, keeping the 9㎛ stratum corneum intact. To finish, pat a few drops of a botanical distillate like bamboo water onto your parting to lock in hydration.

### Q. What should I do if my scalp remains painfully dry and continues to flake?

**A.** If symptoms persist or worsen, consulting a dermatologist is highly recommended. Chronic flaking and persistent tightness can be signs of eczema or psoriasis, which require professional diagnosis.
