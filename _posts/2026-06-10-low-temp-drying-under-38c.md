You wash your hair and plug in your hair dryer. To save time, you set it to the maximum heat and blast your roots. While your hair dries quickly, your scalp feels uncomfortably hot and dry. Soon, a tight, pulling sensation spreads across your crown, leaving your scalp feeling stiff and sensitized.

To speed up their routines, many people dry their hair with intense, high-heat settings. They believe wet hair is the only source of scalp problems. This is a common mistake. Exposing the delicate scalp to high thermal stress acts as a severe dehydrator, baking away the protective moisture barrier and causing structural tension in the skin tissues.

The scalp is a biological layer of living cells, not an inert fabric to be heat-treated. It requires moderate temperatures to preserve its hydration and structural elasticity.

This drying, tight feeling is the result of scalp thermal stress. High heat speeds up transepidermal water loss (TEWL) and causes the scalp's delicate collagen fibers to stiffen. Keeping the drying temperature strictly under 38°C (using the cool or low-warm setting) protects the scalp barrier. Low-temperature drying gently removes excess water from the hair shaft while keeping the stratum corneum hydrated and preserving the elastic, supportive structure of the follicles.
