You sit at your vanity after a warm shower, pulling a standard plastic hairbrush through your locks. Each stroke feels somewhat satisfying, yet a subtle stinging sensation lingers on your crown. By mid-afternoon, that sting transforms into an annoying, persistent itch that makes you want to scratch. You wonder why your scalp feels so reactive, even after using mild shampoos.

To address this irritation, many people buy expensive scalp scaling treatments or wash their hair multiple times a day to clear "dead cells." They believe heavy brushing exfoliation is the key to cleanliness. This is a common misconception. Using rigid plastic bristles with sharp ball-tips behaves like a coarse abrasive on the scalp, scratching the thin epidermal lipid barrier and exposing deep tissue to environmental stress.

The scalp is not a thick leather surface; it is a highly delicate skin ecosystem. Its outermost shield, the stratum corneum, is surprisingly thin. Applying hard plastic tips with high mechanical pressure tears this protective boundary, triggering micro-inflammation.

Biologically, the scalp's stratum corneum is only about 9㎛ thick—significantly thinner and more fragile than facial skin. When you drag a rigid plastic brush across it, the physical stress creates tiny micro-abrasions. These invisible tears allow essential moisture to escape rapidly, leading to dehydration, dryness, and a defensive overproduction of sebum. Furthermore, if symptoms persist or worsen, consulting a dermatologist is highly recommended. To protect these cellular junctions, switching to wide-spaced, soft wooden teeth or using only the soft cushions of your fingertips to untangle hair preserves the lipid structure.
